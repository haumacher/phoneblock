PhoneBlock Dongle

An intelligent answering machine in the guise of an IP phone. The size of a thumb, it plugs into the router's USB port, registers as an additional SIP phone - and intercepts commercial calls before your actual phone rings. Works regardless of the router model, even without a Fritz!

Status: Beta. The first version is already running reliably. I am looking for more testers to try out the dongle in as many different environments as possible, see below.

The idea in one sentence

The dongle logs on to your router like an additional IP phone. Every time a call comes in, it asks the PhoneBlock database at lightning speed: "Is this spam?" If so, it answers the call itself, plays a short announcement if desired and hangs up again. Your real phone has not even rung at that moment. In contrast to the cloud answering machine, all call processing takes place locally at your home.

1st call comes in

The router signals the call to all registered telephones - including the dongle. The "real" telephones can be configured with a ring delay of 3-5 seconds.

2. flash check

The dongle asks PhoneBlock at lightning speed whether the number is known to be spam. The query runs via encrypted HTTPS and only transmits a hash of the number. Calls from contacts in your phone book are not even checked - they never leave your home network.

3. get rid of it

If the number is spam, the dongle picks up, plays an announcement and hangs up. The telephone in the living room remains silent. If the number is not known spam, the dongle simply does nothing - the call goes through normally.

Why this is so charming: The dongle only needs minimal power and WLAN. No additional server, no Fritz!Box, no contract. The ESP32 microcontroller inside draws less than half a watt - you can simply plug it into the USB port of your router and forget about it.

Comparison: Three ways to block landline spam

All three solutions use the same spam database maintained by PhoneBlock users, but differ significantly in terms of effort, effectiveness and the requirements for your connection.

Which solution suits you?

  • Dongle - if you want optimum SPAM protection with the best data protection.
  • Answer Bot - you have your own Fritz!Box (not a rental box) and accept that all your calls are checked by the PhoneBlock server for optimal SPAM defense.
  • Blocklist phonebook - if you have a Fritz!Box (also a rental box), and you don't mind if a spammer slips through every now and then who is actually on the blocklist.

Prerequisites

  • SIP registrar - either in the router or directly with the telephony provider, provided the external SIP clients are permitted ("Registration of a VOIP telephone possible").
  • 2.4 GHz WLAN within range.
  • 5 V USB power supply - ideally USB port in the router, or any USB power supply unit.
  • Free PhoneBlock account.

Contribute experience report

The dongle runs reliably on a Fritz! box in my home - but router models, providers and connection types are a mixed bag, and each combination has its own peculiarities: different SIP dialects, different NAT rules, different ring delay options. To help me recognize patterns and fix problems, structured experience reports are worth their weight in gold.

Setups that are not yet well covered are particularly interesting:

  • Telekom connection with Speedport router
  • Vodafone/Unitymedia cable connection with the supplied provider box
  • 1&1, o2, Deutsche Glasfaser and other providers
  • International connections (Switzerland, Austria, rest of Europe)

Reports are run via GitHub issues - a pre-filled form asks for the most important key data (hardware, router, provider, firmware version, findings) in a structured manner. Other testers can see directly which setups are already covered and which are not.

Build it yourself

The dongle is completely open source. If you are ESP32-savvy (or want to become one), you can build one yourself and get started right away - the material costs are around 8-12 €, depending on where you order the parts.

Hardware

A commercially available ESP32 DevKit with ESP-WROOM-32E module (e.g. NodeMCU-32E) is sufficient. No soldering required - simply connect to the router with a short USB cable. Detailed module and board purchase instructions can be found in the repo.

Firmware

Open source firmware based on ESP-IDF. Flash directly in the Chrome or Edge browser via USB - or classically with the ESP-IDF toolchain. The complete initial configuration (WLAN, SIP, token) runs via a small web frontend directly on the dongle.
